The Brief

Turn the performers into the content.

The Blues Kitchen runs live music seven nights a week across four London venues — it’s the core of the brand. But scanning the feed, the artists who fill those rooms barely featured. The food, the bourbon, the club nights — all well represented. The musicians? The one thing no competitor could replicate? Quiet.

During my internship with the Columbo Group marketing team, I identified this gap and developed Setlist Selects — a short-form vertical video series profiling resident performers through three consistent story beats, designed to be shot quickly, cross-posted across Reels and TikTok, and measurable all the way to booked covers.

Format
  • Vertical 9:16 Reels & TikTok
  • 60s hero + 3–4 cut-downs
  • Captions burned in for silent autoplay
Rollout
  • Phase 1: 3-episode pilot, 1 venue
  • Phase 2: fortnightly across all 4
  • Phase 3: paid amplification & guest names
Platforms
  • Instagram Reels (primary)
  • TikTok (discovery & reach)
  • YouTube & email (Phase 3)
The Insight

Likes are not the win.

The campaign brief came out of a clear data observation: story-led content — artist interviews, behind-the-scenes moments — consistently drives the engagement actions that actually matter to the algorithm and to the business. Not likes. Saves. Shares. Comments.

A like is a reflex. A save is intent to return. A share is free reach to a new audience. And a comment is a conversation. Analysis of content performance by type showed artist stories produced the widest gap between surface likes and deep engagement — that’s where the format lives.

The PEST context made the case stronger: squeezed discretionary spend means every night out has to justify itself, and free, emotionally resonant content lowers the consideration barrier. Meanwhile, TikTok’s algorithm actively rewards the authentic, human stories that live music venues are uniquely positioned to tell.

The Format

One artist. One story. Three beats.

Every episode follows the same structure — instantly recognisable, endlessly repeatable across the full roster of resident performers.

01
The Roots

Where they’re from and how they found this music. The human origin story that audiences connect with before a single note plays.

02
The Record

The one track that changed everything for them — played, hummed, or spun on the night. A shareable, specific, personal moment.

03
The Ritual

What they do in the green room before they walk on stage. The intimate, unseen moment that makes a viewer feel like an insider.

The episode structure opens cold — a scroll-stopping hook line, no intro — then credits the artist with a red lower-third, carries through Roots → Record → Ritual tightly to camera, cuts to a live performance moment with real venue sound, and closes to a CTA driving to the next gig. One weekend shoot yields a 60-second hero Reel plus three or four shorter cut-downs, all cross-posted to IG and TikTok.

The Growth Case

From scroll to seat.

The pitch positioned every episode as an acquisition asset, not a brand post. The funnel runs from vertical reach into profile visits, profile visits into follows, follows into the email and ticketing list, and that list into measurable footfall and booked covers.

Specific target figures are set against three KPIs: six-month follower lift, saves-plus-shares per 1k views (the campaign’s north-star metric over likes), and attributable bookings per month. These were presented to the marketing team with placeholder values to be validated against real CRM and ticketing data before the pilot launched.

The competitive positioning argument was simple: Blues Kitchen already owns a rare spot — credibly music-led but warm and accessible. No competitor in the London live-music landscape was telling the artist stories behind their rooms. That lane was empty.
