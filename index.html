Quantitative Methods · City, University of London · 2025

Loneliness, Class & Psychological Distress

Understanding Society Wave 15 — N = 27,147

A quantitative analysis of the question: “To what extent do socioeconomic positioning and social isolation predict variations in psychological distress amongst UK adults?” Using Understanding Society Wave 15 (N = 27,147, probability-weighted), the study applies OLS regression on the GHQ-12 continuous scale and binary logistic regression for clinical caseness, supported by Pearson correlations, chi-square, and independent samples t-tests in SPSS.

The headline finding challenges policy orthodoxy: loneliness eclipses socioeconomic factors as a predictor of distress — dwarfing the effect of employment status (2.8-point GHQ gap) and making education gradient negligible. The data argues for a reframing of mental health policy around connection, not just economic deprivation.

BSc Dissertation · City, University of London · 2026

Algorithmic Curation & The Public Sphere

How platform algorithms shape political attitudes among young UK adults

A quantitative study examining how algorithmic curation on X (formerly Twitter) mediates news exposure and shapes political attitudes among UK adults aged 18–30. Drawing on Habermas’s public sphere theory, filter bubble research, and algorithmic gatekeeping literature, the study applies hierarchical multiple regression and Pearson correlation analysis across a UK survey sample.

The study interrogates whether personalised algorithmic feeds narrow political exposure or reinforce prior attitudes — with implications for democratic discourse and civic participation among digitally native young adults.
